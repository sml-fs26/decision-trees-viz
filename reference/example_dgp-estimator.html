Stage 1

A new cohort, and a new researcher

A fresh semester, three new students. We know their study hours and lectures attended, but not yet their exam scores:

  • Charlie: 2.5 hours studied, 11 lectures
  • David: 1.8 hours, 8 lectures
  • Evelyn: 3.5 hours, 13 lectures

What will they score on the exam?

Meet Dr. Gauss. He just arrived at the university. His assignment: a dataset of 150 past students — hours studied, lectures attended, final scores — and a simple job, predict the new cohort's scores. He's curious, methodical, and a fan of starting simple.

"Before I use every variable at once," he says, "let me just use X₂ — lectures attended. It's well-behaved; its noise is uniform, unlike the trickier ε on Y. Let's see how far one variable gets us."

Stage 2

Fit the line by hand

Here's the scatter of 150 past students — X₂ (lectures) on the x-axis, Y (exam score) on the y-axis. We want to draw a line through them:

\( Y = a + b \cdot X_2 \)

Two knobs: intercept a and slope b. Turn them. The red line moves. Thin red sticks from each point to the line are the residuals: how far off the line is for each student.

Below the plot, a big red number tracks the sum of squared residuals — add up (residual)² for all 150 students. Your job: make that sum as small as you can.

Stage 3

What you were minimising has a name

That sum you were trying to shrink has a name. It's called the loss function:

\( \mathcal{L}(a, b) \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{n} \bigl( y_i - (a + b\, x_i) \bigr)^2 \)

Turning knobs is one way to minimise it. It's also an awful way. Dr. Gauss doesn't do that.

"The loss is smooth and quadratic. I can take the derivative with respect to a and b, set both to zero, and solve. The answer is a closed formula — no turning knobs needed."

The formula (details of the derivation are for the lecture — trust that it comes out of the two zero-derivative equations) is:

\( \hat{\beta} \;=\; (X^\top X)^{-1} X^\top y \)

The vector \( \hat{\beta} = (\hat{a}, \hat{b}) \) contains the exact numbers that minimise the loss. On the right, you can see your best manual fit on the left half of the plot, and Dr. Gauss's closed-form OLS fit on the right half. Compare the SSRs.

This stage introduced:Loss function \( \mathcal{L}(a,b) \); OLS closed form \( \hat{\beta} = (X^\top X)^{-1} X^\top y \).
Stage 4

Why stop at one variable?

X₂ alone isn't the whole story. Hours studied (X₁) also matters. Dr. Gauss extends the loss to two predictors:

\( \mathcal{L}(a, b_1, b_2) \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{n} \bigl( y_i - (a + b_1 x_{1i} + b_2 x_{2i}) \bigr)^2 \)

Same idea — pick the coefficients that make the gap squared, summed, as small as possible. Only now the "line" is a plane floating in (X₁, X₂, Y) space.

The closed-form answer is the same formula, \( \hat{\beta} = (X^\top X)^{-1} X^\top y \); it just returns three numbers instead of two, because X now has three columns (intercept, X₁, X₂).

On the right: Dr. Gauss's fitted plane, and Charlie, David, Evelyn placed on it. Their predicted scores \( \hat Y \) are labelled — look up from each student's (X₁, X₂) to read off the height of the plane.

This stage introduced:Multiple regression: same loss, same formula, more columns in X.
Stage 5

How sure is Dr. Gauss?

Dr. Gauss reads off \( \hat b_2 \approx 4.7 \). Is that really the effect of an extra lecture? What if he'd collected a different 150 students?

Click "Resample" a few times. Each click regenerates 150 students from the same world, refits the plane, and shows the new \( \hat b_2 \). Watch it wobble.

That spread has a name. The standard error (SE) is the standard deviation of \( \hat b_2 \) across hypothetical resamples.

Dr. Gauss reports it as a one-liner: \( \hat b_2 = 4.70,\; \text{SE} = 0.18,\; \text{95\% CI} = [4.35,\, 5.05] \). The 95% confidence interval means: if we repeated this experiment many times, about 95% of the intervals we compute would cover the population value that \( \hat b_2 \) is estimating. (Whether that value equals the true causal effect of an extra lecture is a separate — and, as we'll see on the next page, urgent — question.)

And if someone doubts that X₂ matters at all — that is, claims \( b_2 = 0 \) — the p-value answers: "under that claim, how surprising is it to observe \( \hat b_2 = 4.7 \)?" A small p-value means: very surprising. Dr. Gauss replies, "I doubt \( b_2 = 0 \)."

This stage introduced:Sampling distribution, standard error (SE), confidence interval (CI), p-value.
Stage 6

What a statistical package shows

Dr. Gauss doesn't compute SE and CI by hand. He runs lm(Y ~ X1 + X2) in R, or smf.ols('Y ~ X1 + X2', data).fit() in Python's statsmodels. Here's what comes out, computed from the actual 150 students on the right:

Every column is now a concept you recognise:

  • Estimate = \( \hat\beta \) from the closed form.
  • Std. Error = the spread you just watched across resamples.
  • t-value = Estimate ÷ SE — how many SEs is the estimate from zero?
  • Pr(>|t|) = the p-value under "this coefficient is really 0".
  • R² = fraction of Y's variance the model explains (see the box below for the formula).
What is R²?

R² (the coefficient of determination) is defined as

\( R^2 \;=\; 1 \;-\; \dfrac{\mathrm{SS}_{\text{res}}}{\mathrm{SS}_{\text{tot}}} \;=\; 1 \;-\; \dfrac{\sum_i (y_i - \hat y_i)^2}{\sum_i (y_i - \bar y)^2} \)

where \( \mathrm{SS}_{\text{res}} \) is the sum of squared residuals (what the model leaves unexplained) and \( \mathrm{SS}_{\text{tot}} \) is the total variation around the mean \( \bar y \). An \( R^2 \) of 1 means the model explains everything, 0 means it explains no more than a horizontal line at \( \bar y \), negative values are possible on held-out data when the model does worse than the mean. Adjusted R² penalises R² for each extra predictor, so it doesn't reward you for throwing more variables at the model.

Dr. Gauss is pleased. His predictions look plausible, his standard errors are tight, his R² is high. He reads off \( \hat b_2 \approx 4.7 \) — "an extra lecture is worth nearly five points on the exam" — and thinks he's done. But…

Something is off. Every semester, certain students consistently sit above Dr. Gauss's plane. Others consistently sit below. If the residuals were just noise, they'd flip sign at random — but they don't. There's a ghost in the residuals — and, as we're about to see, it has been secretly inflating his \( \hat b_2 \) the whole time.
