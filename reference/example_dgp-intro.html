Stage 1 / 6
Meet U
Stage 1

Meet Alice and Bob

Meet Alice. Meet Bob. They sit in the same lecture hall. They take the same notes. This semester, both tell you, honestly, that they studied 2 hours a day and attended 10 out of 12 lectures. Then the exam happens. Alice scores 72. Bob scores 58.

Same hours. Same attendance. Fourteen-point gap.

You're going to spend the next ten minutes figuring out why, by building, piece by piece, the hidden machine that produces exam scores in this world.

First, something you won't see in any spreadsheet. Call it U: self-discipline, grit, focus, whatever you like. Alice has a lot of it. Bob has less. It's real, and it's about to start pulling strings.

How was U measured? U is the score each student gave themselves on an Instagram story slider at the start of the semester: "How disciplined are you with your studies? Drag the slider from 0 (not at all) to 1 (very)." The university has not released this data, so the analyst cannot see it. We standardise these scores to mean 0 and standard deviation 1, giving U ~ N(0,1).

U is who they really are, and we will never see it directly.

This stage introduced:\( U \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1) \)
Stage 2

What U does to study hours

U has already moved them. Alice's discipline didn't just sit in her head. It pushed her to study more hours. Bob's lower U pushed him to study fewer. The same hidden trait leaked into what we thought was an independent input.

But U is not the only thing shaping X₁. There is a second term in the equation, η₁. η₁ is the random individual variation: a student's schedule that week, a midterm in another class, whether they caught a cold, whether their flatmate was loud on Tuesday. It's everything that shifts hours studied but isn't captured by U. We model it as mean-zero noise, independent of U.

That violet arrow is the cause (U's pull). The grey shimmer is η₁. Both land in the same number — hours studied — and from the outside, we can't tell them apart.

This stage introduced:\( X_1 = 2 + 0.5\,U + \eta_1,\quad \eta_1 \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 0.5) \)
Stage 3

Two windows onto U

A detective never trusts a single witness. Neither should a model.

We add a second variable: X₂, lectures attended. Same story: U pushes it, and a second independent noise η₂ jitters it. η₂ is what η₁ was, but for attendance: a bus that didn't come, a clashing appointment, a friend who convinced them to skip. Independent of U. Independent of η₁.

A hundred anonymous students rain in. The cloud tilts. Why? Because U is still pulling the strings, on both axes at once. Students with high U study more and attend more. Students with low U do neither. The tilt is U's fingerprint on the data.

Self-discipline is a strong driver of attendance: a one-standard-deviation bump in U pushes a student to attend roughly 1.5 more lectures. \(\eta_2\) — the random jitter around that pull — has variance 2, so attendance still varies plenty even at a fixed level of discipline.

This stage introduced:\( X_2 = 10 + 1.5\,U + \eta_2,\quad \eta_2 \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 2) \)
Stage 4

The outcome (the full DGP arrives)

Now the exam happens. And the machine that produces scores looks like this:

\( Y = 50 + 8\log(X_1) + 3X_2 + 6U + \varepsilon \)

Every term earns its place. The log says the first hour of study is worth much more than the tenth: diminishing returns, baked right in. The 3·X₂ rewards showing up. The 6·U is the direct fingerprint of discipline on the exam itself — and it is big. A one-standard-deviation bump in self-discipline contributes six points to the final score, entirely through channels the analyst never sees: focus during study sessions, the practice problem a disciplined student does anyway, the ability to read a question carefully under time pressure. Hours studied and lectures attended are only part of how U lifts a score; U has a direct line to Y as well.

And ε? ε is the irreducible exam-day noise: a lucky guess on a question, a moment of panic, a pen that ran out, a question that happened to favour one student's intuition. It's what neither U nor X₁ nor X₂ can predict. We'll see its shape in the next stage.

This stage introduced:\( Y = 50 + 8\log(X_1) + 3\,X_2 + 6\,U + \varepsilon \)
Stage 5

The shape of the noise

We wrote \( \varepsilon \) as if it were one uniform cloud. It isn't.

Plot each student's exam-day noise — ε, the leftover after we subtract \(50 + 8\log(X_1) + 3X_2 + 6U\) from their score — against the hours they studied. Instead of a flat band you see a funnel. Students who studied barely at all cluster tightly around zero. Students who studied a lot fan out wildly, above and below.

This is heteroscedasticity. The noise variance is not a constant; it scales with \( |X_1| \):

\( \varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\; 2\,|X_1|\right) \)

Why this shape? A student who studied 30 minutes can only do so well, and can only do so badly — their score is pinned. A student who studied six hours has more room to move: momentum, fatigue, lucky topic overlap, unlucky question ordering. Their outcomes fan out. The gold curves show ±SD(ε) — the characteristic funnel.

This stage introduced:\( \varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\; 2\,|X_1|\right) \)
Stage 6

One last variable: self-assessment

After the exam, imagine we ask every student: "How do you think you did?" Call their answer X₃: self-assessed performance. It tracks the score closely, because students who did well feel like they did well.

Formally, X₃ is a noisy copy of Y:

\( X_3 = 0.9\,Y + \eta_3 \)

Notice the direction of the arrow. Y causes X₃. X₃ does not cause Y. That makes X₃ a post-treatment variable: measured after the outcome, shaped by it.

The scatter on the right plots X₃ against Y. The slope is roughly 0.9, and the points hug that line tightly. This is exactly what a noisy copy of Y looks like, and that tightness is what will tempt a naive analyst into disaster on the next page.

This stage introduced:\( X_3 = 0.9\,Y + \eta_3 \)

The world is built. U shapes the inputs, the inputs shape Y, and a noisy self-assessment rides along behind. Next: a new cohort arrives, and we ask — what's our best guess for their scores?
