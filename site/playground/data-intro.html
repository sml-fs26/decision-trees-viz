Stage 1

Two students

Meet Alice and Bob. Two students in the same lecture hall.

We measure two things about each of them:

  • \(X_1\): hours studied per week (somewhere between 0 and 10).
  • \(X_2\): lectures attended out of 14.

Alice studies about 3 hours a week and makes it to 6 lectures. Bob studies 7 hours and attends 11. Two dots on a 2D plane. That's it for now — no labels, no rules, no tree.

A student is a point in \( \mathbb{R}^2 \).

Stage 2

A label appears

The semester ends. The exam happens. Each student gets an outcome.

Alice passed. Bob failed. We colour each dot by the outcome: pink for pass, blue for fail.

Now each student has a label. Classification is finding the rule that assigns it.

Two dots isn't much of a pattern. We need more students.

Stage 3

The cohort arrives

150 students spill onto the plot. Each one is still a pair \((X_1, X_2)\), and each one is now coloured by their outcome.

Look for a bit. There is structure — students with more hours and more attendance tend to pass. But it's messy. Pass and fail overlap near the middle. Some students who studied a lot still failed. Some who barely showed up somehow passed.

Real data is noisy. Exam days have lucky breaks and unlucky ones. The label you see is the truth plus a little randomness.

Use the noise level slider below to see how messy it gets.

Stage 4

The true boundary

Secretly, there is a rule. The world was built with one. For this cohort:

\( \text{pass} \iff X_1 + X_2 > 12 \)

We draw it as a gold dashed line. Look how well (and not perfectly) it separates pink from blue.

Somewhere out there, a rule decides the label. Our job is to learn it from the data.

Flip the show true boundary toggle below to hide and reveal the line.

In the real world, you never get to see the gold line. You only see the points. A good classifier recovers something close to the gold line from the dots alone.
Stage 5

The formal setup

Before we learn anything, we fix the language. Straight from the lecture:

Partition. A partition of \( \mathbb{R}^d \) is a tuple \( (S_1, \dots, S_K) \) of disjoint subsets whose union is \( \mathbb{R}^d \):

\[ S_i \cap S_j = \emptyset \text{ for } i \neq j, \qquad S_1 \cup \dots \cup S_K = \mathbb{R}^d. \]

Classification tree. A classification tree is a sequence of pairs

\[ (S_1, \ell_1), \dots, (S_K, \ell_K), \qquad \ell_i \in \{1, \dots, M\}. \]

Its induced function is \( t : \mathbb{R}^d \to \{1, \dots, M\} \) with \( t(x) = \ell_i \) whenever \( x \in S_i \).

Given a dataset \( D = \{(x_1, y_1), \dots, (x_n, y_n)\} \subset \mathbb{R}^d \times \{1, \dots, M\} \), the tree partitions \( D \) into \( D_1, \dots, D_K \), where \( D_i \) holds the points whose \( x \) falls in \( S_i \).

That's the what. Next up: the how. How do we actually carve \( \mathbb{R}^2 \) into regions just by looking at the dots?
