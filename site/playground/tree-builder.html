“Let me classify these students. I'll start with one split — one straight line across the plot. On one side I'll predict passed; on the other, failed. It won't be perfect. But it's a beginning.”

Dr. Quinlan's plan is the seed of every decision tree: pick a feature, pick a threshold, cut the space in two. Do it well and you've already captured a lot of the story. Do it again, recursively, and you've built a tree.

The idea. A classification tree partitions the feature space into axis-aligned rectangles and assigns a label to each one. Every rectangle is reached by a sequence of yes/no questions about the features.

A smoother quality measure: entropy

Counting mistakes is honest, but it's bumpy — moving the line a tiny bit often doesn't change the count at all. A smoother measure of how mixed a region is will serve us better. That measure is entropy:

\[ H(D) = -\sum_{j} p_j \log_2 p_j \]

where \(p_j\) is the fraction of label \(j\) in the region \(D\). Pure region → \(H=0\); perfectly mixed → \(H=1\) (for two classes).

When Dr. Quinlan splits the data with a line \((f, t)\), he gets two children \(D_L\) and \(D_R\). The quality of the split is their weighted-sum entropy:

\[ Q(D, f, t) = \frac{|D_L|}{|D|}\, H(D_L) + \frac{|D_R|}{|D|}\, H(D_R) \]

Slide the threshold again. Now you can read \(H(D_L)\), \(H(D_R)\), and \(Q\) live. Below the scatter, a line plot shows \(Q\) as the threshold sweeps across the axis — the minimum is the best split on this feature.

Now do it again — on each side

A single split is rarely enough. After the first cut, Dr. Quinlan has two datasets \(D_L, D_R\). He applies the same procedure to each. Then again, and again. The splits pile up, the partition refines, and a tree starts to form.

The algorithm, in six lines

Everything you just saw is a recursive greedy procedure. Written out:

def train(D):
    if H(D) == 0:
        return leaf(y)          # pure region — no need to split
    else:
        (f, t) = argmin(f,t) Q(D, f, t)
        D_L, D_R = split D by (f, t)
        return node(f, t, train(D_L), train(D_R))

The base case: if the region is already pure (\(H = 0\)), emit a leaf with its single label. The recursive case: pick the best split, partition the data, and train two subtrees.

That's the whole algorithm. In practice we add a depth limit (or a minimum-leaf-size rule) to the base case — and that's exactly what saves us from the trouble on the next stage.

Without a depth limit: overfitting

Let's see what happens if we let Dr. Quinlan go all the way. Slide the depth up. Training accuracy climbs monotonically, and if you push it high enough, it touches 1.00 — every single training student is classified correctly.

Now look at the test accuracy. A separate cohort of 200 students was generated from the same world (different seed) and held out. On the plot below, the blue line is training accuracy; the pink line is test accuracy.

max depth 3
The trap. Past a certain depth, training accuracy keeps rising but test accuracy falls. Dr. Quinlan isn't learning the rule any more — he's memorising the 5% label noise in the training set. On fresh data that noise is different, and his extra splits actively hurt.
The fix. Cap the depth. A shallow tree can't memorise noise because it doesn't have enough leaves to do so. A depth limit is crude but remarkably effective — the cheapest regulariser trees have.
